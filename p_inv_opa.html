Inverse Opals

Time-based Photosensitive Receptor and Art Applications

This is part of a series of studies on material mechanisms and devices at the nano, micro, and macro scales. Each research includes a set of experiments, simulations, and representations for its associated design speculations.

Harvard Graduate School of Design, SEAS, Fall 2021

Role: Device modeling, fabrications, Sensor integrations, Material processing

Category: Term final project

Team: Caroline Dignes, Gino Domel, Toria Justicz

Advisors: Jonathan Grinham, James C. Weaver

Design Integrations

Due to their ability to self-organize at the nanoscale, inverse opals display a range of iridescent colors, which is commonly referred to as structural color. Using this concept, we first explored using inverse opals as a method for indicating the amount of time that has passed based on light exposure to provide a way for users to receive a regular reminder, such as when to perform a COVID test.

Our proposal for the wearable reminder bracelet within the context of the COVID-19 pandemic was a simple text testing reminder (“Go Test”), which would reveal itself over a period of exposure to light (Figure A&B). In their results, Singleton reveals their axochromophore coated structure becomes hydrophilic between 20 mins and 2 hours. They propose that for other “tamper devices” the light sensitivity would need to be increased to expose faster. Implicitly, this would suggest one could also tune the azo-chromophore to sense UV light more slowly, perhaps allowing a design tuned for one or two weeks. Conversely, our proposal for the children’s drawing board was simply a blank IOF.

Due to constraints, the photosensitive aspect of our design was not able to be prototyped and tested. However, we were able to test how our design reacted to a brushing of both regular water and soapy water to its surface. our dry IOFs refract light wherever the film covers the glass slide.

Inverse opal film wetted with water (left) vs. wetted with soapy water (right).

As soap being a surfactant, which lowers the surface tension of water enough to wet some of the hydrophobic pores which turns the background more clear, making it easier to see the words.

Painting with water on an inverse opal film.

We obtained an inverse opal film on a glass slide with no mask applied, creating an un-encoded IOF. This allowed us to explore the inverse opals as an art tool. Using a brush dipped in water, it was easy to coat specific regions selectively with water to create various shapes.
