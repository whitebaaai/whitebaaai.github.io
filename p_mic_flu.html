Microfluidic Thermoregulations

Temporary Microfluidic Pop-up Structures for Public Thermoregulations

This is part of a series of studies on material mechanisms and devices at the nano, micro, and macro scales. Each research includes a set of experiments, simulations, and representations for its associated design speculations.

Design of Microfluidic device from 2d to 3d PDMS-cast

Concept: Microfluidic Structures for Public Thermoregulation

With small channels and direct flow of liquid, microfluidics can be used to provide heat or offload heat into the atmosphere. For instance, while buildings are made of insulating materials, microfluidic panels provide a means for structural thermoregulation. We then explored the applications of microfluidics for designing temporary structures to exhibit cooling properties within metropolitan settings. (e.g. city heat waves, festivals, etc.). Using microfluidic design principles, we sought to create a geometry that would best allow for efficient heat offloading.

Result

The PDMS devices were heated with a heat lamp to approximately 322 K. As room temperature water was run through the device, an infrared camera was used to record the temperature of the microfluidic device. Both qualitatively and quantitatively, the simulations and testing match well. To more precisely explore this, we used the test data to plot the average surface temperature of the primary region of the device as a function of time.

While the COMSOL simulations were reasonably accurate, we further explored a multitude of designs to determine which performed best. both infrared images show the temperature in additional tests run for one of our spiral iterations. The spiral iterations excel in terms of cooling ability with heat flux values near 100 W m2 relative to 71 W m2 in the simulations. Additionally, the average surface temperature of the spiral tests tended to be around 305 K. This can largely be attributed to the fact that the spiral designs cover a larger surface area, which naturally means a larger magnitude of the heat flux and lower temperatures at steady state.

Time-dependent Simulations

At a low flow rate, COMSOL was performed for the average surface temperature. The curves show the comparison between this simulation and the test data. The quantitative difference between the low flow rate testing data and the COMSOL simulation can be attributed to 1. the region used for the average temperature over a given area is slightly different due to molding. 2. we readjusted our initial device at the location of the outlet due to minor fabrication issues.

Concept sketch for deployable mobile structures attached to a fire hydrant within a metropolitan setting

Design Speculations

This research provides an interesting direction for creating cooling, temporary structures that could provide a critical role in safety and comfort during heat waves. By 1. attaching the inlet of the structure to city fire hydrants (which access underground water that is stored at a cooler temperature than traditional tap water) and 2. pairing this with a unique microfluidic geometry that optimally offloads heat, we can create spaces that safely shelter individuals during times of extreme heat.
